Problem we set out to solve

Most of Defra's forms were paper-based, making them inaccessible, costly, and inefficient. The existing digital tools available for building forms were unintuitive, inaccessible, and required significant training, limiting the pace and efficiency of digitisation efforts.

Traditionally, many interactions with Defra could only be completed via offline or manual channels such as call centres, email, or paper/PDF forms. These channels are not only expensive to operate, but also lead to unstructured and error-prone data collection, making it difficult to process information efficiently and accurately.

  • Call centres: High operational costs and resource-intensive
  • Email: Unstructured submissions, hard to automate or analyse
  • Paper/PDF: Prone to manual errors, slow to process, and inaccessible for many users

We started with the x-gov form builder, which didn't meet accessibility requirements and wasn't very intuitive. This has prevented the team from opening the product to other content designers to help tackle the backlog of forms and new requests coming in. We need to make DXT more intuitive, user friendly and accessible to aid adoption.

However, although starting with x-gov form builder (starting with what already exists), we needed to make the product more intuitive, user friendly and accessible to aid adoption.

This is because usage of the x-gov form builder impacted:

  • User confidence
  • Form publication speed
  • Widespread adoption by content designers outside the core Defra Forms team
  • Ability to meet WCAG 2.2 compliance requirements
  • Conformance to design standards

Defra is a large and complex organisation, working with over 35 agencies and public bodies, each with different policies, operations, and technical systems. This diversity means that a one-size-fits-all approach does not work, and any solution must be flexible enough to support a wide range of needs and service teams.

The following sections highlight the work we've done to improve the x-gov form builder, and the new forms designer (version: Titan).

Mapping out the current state (as-is)

We mapped the existing x-gov form builder workflows extensively using detailed diagrams in Figma. This allowed us to pinpoint specific areas for improvement and establish baseline metrics for future iterations.

Key findings included:

  • A complex, unintuitive interface
  • Overwhelming information presentation
  • Non-standardised components causing user confusion
  • Accessibility barriers preventing wider adoption
  • Inconsistent design patterns

Initial assessment: Accessibility and heuristic evaluation

To identify immediate usability issues, we started with heuristic and accessibility testing of the existing x-gov form builder. A senior interaction designer and two senior content designers evaluated the product against NNGroup heuristics and accessibility guidelines.

Findings highlighted multiple usability and accessibility concerns, documented clearly in our Design History.

We also engaged the Defra accessibility team for an independent review.

Key Changes and Improvements

Through 13 rounds of user research conducted between May 2024 and April 2025, involving over 50 participants including content designers, proxy users, and internal stakeholders across Defra departments, we identified and addressed several key areas for improvement in the Forms Designer. Here's how we transformed the experience:

Summary of improvements

  • From component-led to intent-led: Users begin by declaring what kind of page they need (Question vs Guidance), rather than hunting through a dense catalogue of components
  • Linear, guided editing: Designers move through steps with clear progression, replacing the modal-heavy approach of the current builder
  • Multi-question pages: Support for adding multiple questions within a single page, with intuitive reordering
  • Consolidated page management: All page-level actions consolidated into a single Page overview screen
  • Contextual choices: Options are revealed progressively based on user intent, reducing cognitive load
  • Clear save points: Explicit save actions and visual feedback on changes

The redesign replaces a component-driven, tool-heavy experience with a task-driven, guided approach that dramatically reduces complexity and cognitive load.

The new system provides clear separation of concerns between form, page, and question levels, with explicit save points and contextual controls that appear only when needed.

The new editor introduces a dedicated Conditions tab and a powerful Conditions manager. Users can add conditions directly from the page overview or manage all conditions centrally. The journey is visual and step-by-step: you select a question, choose a plain English condition type (like 'is' or 'is not'), and pick a value. You can add multiple conditions using AND/OR logic, preview the logic in real time, and see a summary of all conditions applied to each page. Named conditions can be reused across the form, and the Conditions manager provides a clear overview of where each condition is used, making maintenance and updates much easier. This approach dramatically reduces complexity, prevents errors, and gives content designers confidence and control over form logic.

Listing page condition filter: The listing page now includes a powerful filter that lets users quickly find and view all pages with conditions applied. You can filter the list to show only pages with specific conditions, making it easy to audit logic, troubleshoot complex forms, and ensure that all necessary routing is in place. This feature is especially valuable for large forms, giving content designers a clear overview and direct access to pages with conditional logic.

Finding and managing forms

Old iteration (finding forms):

  • no way to distinguish between form that were being worked on and those that were live
  • in a long list of forms, it was hard to find the one you were looking for because you could only go off the name of the form

    • Latest iteration (finding forms):

    • now you can see the status of the form
    • you can search and filter a form by name, status, or date
    • you can filter forms by status, date, or name
    • there's a clear and familiar process for publishing forms
    • you can publish a form without an engineer
    • you can add meta data to a form

    "'Search' works very well – it meets what users need." — Content designer feedback from Rounds 3 & 4

    Scope and aims of the service

    Our goal was clear: transform the x-gov form builder into a timely, cost-effective, accessible, and user-centred digital experience for content designers building online forms. This would streamline digitisation and enable rapid, accurate processing of data submitted by end users.

    Key objectives included:

    • Achieve WCAG 2.2 compliance across all components and interactions
    • Conform to design standards and best practices
    • Improve intuitiveness and user experience
    • Enable wider adoption by content designers
    • Accelerate form digitisation efforts

    Research and Iterations

    Our redesign process was driven by extensive user research and iterative testing across 13 rounds between May 2024 and April 2025, involving over 50 participants from content designers, proxy users, and internal stakeholders across Defra departments.

    Key Themes and Trends

    • Form Creation Flow: Users preferred a linear, intuitive flow from form name to question creation
    • Page Conditions: Improved terminology from "operator" to "condition type" and better visibility of condition filters
    • Multiple Response Questions: Renamed from "repeater" to "multiple responses" for clarity
    • Visual Design: Positive feedback on GOV.UK style familiarity and white space usage
    • Guidance Tools: Enhanced markdown support and contextual help integration
    • Accessibility: Focus on WCAG 2.2 compliance and inclusive design patterns

    "Even just doing it once made it less confusing the second time." — Content designer feedback from Round 2

    Research Journey

    Our research journey spanned 13 rounds, each building upon previous findings and driving continuous improvements:

    Initial Discovery (Rounds 1-3)

    • Initial research with 6 content designers from various Defra teams
    • Key findings on form library, filters, and terminology clarity
    • Identified need for better search functionality and immediate access to personal forms
    • Clarified confusion around "Published" vs "Live" terminology
    • Component testing showed improved usability with new designs
    • Users responded positively to clearer visual feedback
    • User journey testing confirmed improved navigation and task success rates

    Deep Dive and Refinement (Rounds 4-7)

    • Explored complex interactions like conditional logic and multiple responses
    • Refined terminology and improved guidance clarity
    • Enhanced accessibility features based on user feedback
    • Implemented enhanced component designs with progressive enhancement support

    Validation and Optimization (Rounds 8-10)

    • Tested complete user journeys with diverse user groups
    • Optimized performance and interaction patterns
    • Further refinement of navigation hierarchy implemented

    Final Iterations (Rounds 11-13)

    • Conducted comprehensive accessibility testing
    • Refined edge cases and complex scenarios
    • Validated improvements with new and returning users

    Key Research Outcomes

    • Improved navigation hierarchy and clarity
    • Enhanced component accessibility
    • Streamlined user workflows
    • Better progressive enhancement support
    • Clearer visual feedback
    • Standardised UI patterns
    • Reduced cognitive load
    • Improved task success rates

    "The design feels really clean, really like a lot of space, very methodical step by step" — Content designer feedback

    Overall Flow and Page Types

    The Forms Designer follows a clear, intuitive flow that guides content designers through the form creation process. The service is built with progressive enhancement principles, ensuring a quality experience for all users, regardless of JavaScript availability.

    Form Management Pages

    • Form Listing Page: Summary of all created pages and end pages (including Check answers page), with quick preview functionality when JavaScript is enabled
    • Page Re-ordering: Intuitive interface for changing page order using up and down controls
    • Form Preview: Complete preview of the form from start to finish

    Form Creation Pages

    • Page Type Selection: Choose between question or guidance page creation
    • Information Type Selection: Select the specific type of question to add
    • Question Configuration: Configure individual questions with appropriate settings
    • Page Overview: Summary of questions, page heading, guidance, multiple response settings, and question reordering

    Progressive Enhancement Features

    The service is designed to work effectively with or without JavaScript, with specific enhancements for JavaScript-enabled environments:

    JavaScript Disabled

    • Preview panels shown separately from configuration panels
    • Preview buttons for viewing questions/pages or error messages
    • Page reloads required for reordering actions

    JavaScript Enabled

    • Preview panels shown alongside configuration panels
    • Skip links for quick navigation between panels (visible on tab focus)
    • Left-border highlighting for questions in preview panel
    • Guided input with dynamic highlighting of related preview content
    • Smooth reordering without page reloads
    • Focus-based highlighting of corresponding preview elements

    Reordering Features

    The service includes specialized reordering functionality for different elements:

    • Lists (checkboxes, radios)
    • Pages
    • Questions

    All reordering movements include specific screen reader announcements for accessibility.

    Outcomes and next steps

    Our iterative redesign led to tangible improvements:

    • Users quickly recognised navigation elements and could intuitively interact with the interface
    • Clearer UI reduced cognitive load, improved confidence, and increased task success rates
    • Improved component accessibility and usability, accelerating form creation and publication

    Next Steps

    • Continue refining terminology (e.g., 'repeater', 'short description')
    • Improve layout clarity for sections and condition logic
    • Support card sorts and diary studies to enhance guidance IA
    • Conduct accessibility testing and continue inclusive design
