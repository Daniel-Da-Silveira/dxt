Problem we set out to solve

Most of Defra's forms were paper-based, making them inaccessible, costly, and inefficient. The existing digital tools available for building forms were unintuitive, inaccessible, and required significant training, limiting the pace and efficiency of digitisation efforts.

Traditionally, many interactions with Defra could only be completed via offline or manual channels such as call centres, email, or paper/PDF forms. These channels are not only expensive to operate, but also lead to unstructured and error-prone data collection, making it difficult to process information efficiently and accurately.

  • Call centres: High operational costs and resource-intensive
  • Email: Unstructured submissions, hard to automate or analyse
  • Paper/PDF: Prone to manual errors, slow to process, and inaccessible for many users

We started with the x-gov form builder, which didn't meet accessibility requirements and wasn't very intuitive. This has prevented the team from opening the product to other content designers to help tackle the backlog of forms and new requests coming in. We need to make DXT more intuitive, user friendly and accessible to aid adoption.

However, although starting with x-gov form builder (starting with what already exists), we needed to make the product more intuitive, user friendly and accessible to aid adoption.

This is because usage of the x-gov form builder impacted:

  • User confidence
  • Form publication speed
  • Widespread adoption by content designers outside the core Defra Forms team
  • Ability to meet WCAG 2.2 compliance requirements
  • Conformance to design standards

Defra is a large and complex organisation, working with over 35 agencies and public bodies, each with different policies, operations, and technical systems. This diversity means that a one-size-fits-all approach does not work, and any solution must be flexible enough to support a wide range of needs and service teams.

The following sections highlight the work we've done to improve the x-gov form builder, and the new forms designer (version: Titan).

Mapping out the current state (as-is)

We mapped the existing x-gov form builder workflows extensively using detailed diagrams in Figma. This allowed us to pinpoint specific areas for improvement and establish baseline metrics for future iterations.

Key findings included:

  • A complex, unintuitive interface
  • Overwhelming information presentation
  • Non-standardised components causing user confusion
  • Accessibility barriers preventing wider adoption
  • Inconsistent design patterns

Initial assessment: Accessibility and heuristic evaluation

To identify immediate usability issues, we started with heuristic and accessibility testing of the existing x-gov form builder. A senior interaction designer and two senior content designers evaluated the product against NNGroup heuristics and accessibility guidelines.

Findings highlighted multiple usability and accessibility concerns, documented clearly in our Design History.

We also engaged the Defra accessibility team for an independent review.
