Approximately 1 in 4 people (24%) in the UK have a disability or long-term impairment.

This stat does not include:

  • unreported disabilities
  • neurodiversity (such as Autism or ADHD)
  • temporary impairments (for example, a person with an arm injury)
  • situational impairments (for example, a new parent holding a baby)

You can estimate how many disabled people might use your form.

Common usability problems in forms

  • Length and complexity
  • Inconsistent design
  • Confusing error messages
  • Getting timed out
  • Lack of guidance or examples
  • Unhelpful hint text
  • Poor mobile optimisation
  • Expectations not managed

Different access needs can clash

Consider this scenario.

User 1 needs all the information on one page. They'll forget what they have read if they need to switch between pages.

User 2 finds all the information on one page overwhelming. They need to have separate pages that break up the information.

It's tricky to cater to both users in this instance. Do what you can, with the information you have and the technology available.

Chloe Tear's blog has more information on what to do when different access needs clash.

Making accessible forms

Since 23 September 2018 all public sector websites and apps need to meet accessibility standards and publish an accessibility statement. Online forms also need to comply with the standards.

Stick to tried and tested methods when designing a form.

You should also:

  • write questions and guidance in plain English
  • make things as simple as possible
  • write clear start pages (how users will find your form on GOV.UK) or update existing pages and qualifying information
  • bookmark the Defra accessibility intranet page
  • check the guidance for structuring forms

Accessibility statement for forms

If you use a government form building tool like the Defra Form Designer or GOV.UK Forms, your form is usually covered by the tool's accessibility statement.

Assistive technology

Some people might use assistive technology to complete online forms. You can:

  • learn about assistive technology
  • research your form with disabled people and assistive technology users
  • ask the Defra accessibility team to audit your form, you can email them at accessibility@defra.gov.uk

If you need help testing the accessibility of your form, contact the quality, assurance and test team at qatestresourcing@defra.gov.uk.

Asking for personal information

Consider the sensitives around personal questions. 'Easy' questions might be hard for some people to answer. Don't assume people can complete a form independently. They may need help from:

  • friends and family
  • teachers or social workers
  • charities and citizens advice
  • farm management companies
  • accountants

If asking for personal data is a legal requirement, ask only for what the law requires.

Learn more about trauma informed practice.

Assisted digital

Some people might need help getting online.

Helping people use your online form is known as providing 'assisted digital support'.

You can provide assisted digital support:

  • in person
  • on the telephone
  • via webchat

You should give users information about assisted digital support via:

  • your online service - for example on a GOV.UK start page or within your form
  • other communications - letters, emails, posters, leaflets, social media, forums, radio advertising, online advertising, third party face to face support channels

Read more about providing assisted digital support.

When to use a paper form

Offer a paper version of an online form if your research suggests users need it. If you don't have any research, you can do desk research to find articles like why people prefer to use paper forms.

Review the paper form for accessibility. Use the accessible form template for forms in Word. Check the guidance for making accessible PDFs.
