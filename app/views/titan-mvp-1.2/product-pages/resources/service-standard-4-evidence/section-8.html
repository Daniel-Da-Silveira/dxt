8. How data is used to understand service performance

Example 1: Research Data Collection and Analysis

Situation:
Need systematic way to collect, analyse, and act on user research data to understand service performance.

Task:
Document research findings systematically and use data to inform design decisions.

Action:

  • Documented all research rounds with: research objectives, participants, methods, findings, quotes, recommendations
  • Captured user quotes in 17 features (52%)
  • Documented findings in 28 features (85%)
  • Tracked issues and recommendations
  • Used research data to prioritise improvements

Result:

  • Systematic documentation of all research
  • Data available to inform design decisions
  • Clear evidence of why changes were made
  • 77 research iterations documented across 32 features

Example 2: Using Performance Metrics to Understand User Feedback

Situation:
A user got in touch after being locked out of the "save and exit" feature on a long, complex application form. They'd carefully noted down their password, but when they tried to get back in the system wouldn't let them. Losing progress on something that takes hours to fill in is understandably very frustrating, and it kicked off a discussion in the team about how this could happen and whether it was a wider issue.

Task:
Understand whether this was an isolated incident or a wider problem affecting multiple users, and determine the best approach to address it.

Action:

  • Rather than guessing, the team looked at the logs to see what was really happening
  • On the same day this complaint came in, found that 94 people had successfully resumed a saved form
  • Over the past week, only six people had been locked out after too many failed password attempts
  • Calculated the success rate: approximately 96% of users successfully use the feature
  • Recognised that while the feature works smoothly most of the time, when it goes wrong the consequences for users are significant
  • Used both the user's story and the data side by side to get a rounded picture of the problem

Result:

  • Data showed the feature works smoothly most of the time (about a 96% success rate)
  • Identified that when it goes wrong, the consequences for users are big
  • With that context, the conversation moved to how to make things better
  • Generated ideas for improvements including: clearer warnings about case sensitivity, better messaging before someone runs out of attempts, a reset option, and considering alternatives like one-time codes instead of passwords
  • Demonstrated how feedback and data together can shape the next steps for service improvement
